# A Note to Tapiwa and Misheck

_From Koen and Justice — March 2026_

---

Thank you for being part of this. What we are trying to do together is genuinely important — giving children in rural Zimbabwe access to learning tools that most schools in the world take for granted. You are the people who make that real on the ground. We cannot do this without you.

This note explains what we are asking of each of you, and how we work together as a team.

---

## What IDEA is doing

IDEA is a charity based in Belgium. We have installed offline computer systems at three sites: Monde Primary School, Ndlovu Secondary School, and Umhambi Orphanage. These systems run without internet — everything works from a local server in the room.

The system has three main tools: **Kolibri** (educational lessons and quizzes), **Nextcloud** (file sharing and communication), and **Wikipedia** (an offline encyclopaedia). Teachers do not need to know much about computers to use them — that is what the training is for.

Our goal for the first six weeks is simple: by the end, at least two teachers at each site can use the system independently with their students. Everything we do during visits is in service of that goal.

---

## Tapiwa — what we are asking of you

You are the technical lead. You are the only person on the ground who understands the full system, and that makes you essential.

At every visit, you do three things: check that the system is running, train the teachers, and check whether students are actually using it. The training follows a curriculum — one topic per week, building from the basics in Week 1 to real classroom use by Week 4. You have the presentations to guide each session.

Do not rush the training. If a teacher is not ready to move to the next topic, repeat the current one and note why in the report. A teacher who has genuinely learned something slowly is worth more than one who has been moved through topics they did not understand.

You are also the first line of technical support. If something is wrong with the system, you fix it. If you cannot fix it on-site, Misheck posts in the WhatsApp group and Koen provides support remotely. The troubleshooting guide covers the most common problems — keep it with you.

---

## Misheck — what we are asking of you

You are the person who holds the visits together and keeps the relationship with the schools healthy.

You organise every visit — transport, timing, making sure the right people are there. During training sessions, you observe: which teachers are engaged, which are struggling, who is absent. Those observations go into the weekly field report. They are often the most valuable part — Koen and Justice cannot see what you see.

You are also the first point of contact when something goes wrong with a school relationship. You know the people, the context, and the community. When a teacher stops showing up or a school contact becomes unresponsive, you are the one who reaches out directly and finds out what is happening.

For Umhambi Orphanage, you are both the field coordinator and the head of the institution. We trust your judgment on how to handle things there.

---

## How we work together

Every week after visits, you write one field report and send it to the WhatsApp group. The format is simple — there is a template. Koen reviews the technical side; Justice reviews the teaching progress. Within 48 hours, one of us will respond with any questions or adjustments for the following week.

Payment of $70 is released by Koen when the field report is received. This covers fuel and your time. If a visit has to be missed, tell us in advance — we will discuss it.

Koen and Justice read everything in the WhatsApp group. If you have a question, a problem, or something you noticed that does not fit in the report, post it there. We are following along and we will respond.

---

## What we expect by the end of six weeks

At each school: the system is running, teachers have accounts set up in both Kolibri and Nextcloud, at least two teachers can use the tools independently with their students, and students are logging in and engaging with the content.

We know this takes real effort. Schools are busy. Teachers are new to computers. Things will go wrong and need to be fixed. That is normal — what matters is that you keep going, keep observing, and keep telling us what is happening so we can support you from Belgium.

We are glad to have you on the team.

**Koen & Justice**
